Leassh vs Bark: The macOS and Local-First Gap

Published April 16, 2026 | By Carl-Gustav Öberg

I was talking with a parent last week who had just discovered something alarming: the parental control service they'd been paying for doesn't work on their Mac.

Their teenager had gotten a MacBook for school. The family had Bark installed on Windows desktops and iPhones. But when they tried to set up Bark on the Mac, they found... nothing. No macOS download. No macOS app. Just a dead end.

This isn't an isolated incident. I've now spoken with at least a dozen families who discovered this same gap after purchasing Bark subscriptions. They're paying for family protection that simply doesn't cover one of their devices.

The macOS Gap Bark Doesn't Acknowledge

Let me be direct about what I found when I investigated Bark's platform support:

Bark has NO native macOS application.

I checked:

  • Bark's download page — no macOS option
  • Bark's pricing page — device tiers but no macOS download links
  • Bark's documentation — confirms Windows and Linux for Bark Home only
  • Apple's App Store — no Bark application

Yet Bark's marketing positions their service as "family protection." How can a service claim to protect your family when it doesn't support one of the most popular family devices?

Here's what Bark officially supports according to their website:

  • Windows — Desktop application available
  • Linux — Bark Home supports Linux
  • iOS — Mobile monitoring available
  • Android — Confirmed support exists
  • macOS — NOT SUPPORTED

This is a fundamental product gap that Bark's marketing doesn't highlight.

Why This Matters for Mac Families

macOS powers a significant portion of family devices. According to 2026 market data:

  • macOS holds approximately 15-17% of the desktop market in the United States
  • Many families use Macs as their primary home computer
  • MacBook Air and MacBook Pro are popular choices for students
  • Parents often use Macs for work and family management

When a parental control service doesn't support macOS, families face several problems:

1. You're paying for features you can't use. If your family has any Mac devices, Bark cannot monitor them. You're subsidizing coverage for devices you can't actually protect.

2. Inconsistent protection across devices. Your teenager might use an iPhone (fully monitored) at school but a Mac (unmonitored) at home. This creates gaps where problems can develop without your knowledge.

3. The trust gap. When you tell your child "I have monitoring set up" but they know you can't see their Mac activity, you've created a false sense of security.

4. Frustration and wasted money. Multiple parents told me they discovered the macOS gap after purchasing Bark subscriptions and attempting setup. By then, they were already committed to the service.

What Bark Actually Does Well

I want to be fair: Bark has earned its reputation for reasons. When I tested their service with devices they do support, I found genuine strengths:

Bark's actual strengths:

  • AI-powered content scanning across 30+ platforms is impressive
  • Correctly identified concerning content in Instagram DMs and Discord messages
  • Strong privacy posture with human review of flagged content
  • Works well on iOS, Windows, and Linux

But here's the problem: For families with Macs, Bark's strengths don't matter because they can't access the Mac devices that often matter most.

The Leassh Alternative: Native Mac Support

When I built Leassh, I was solving a different problem: parental control services that don't actually cover the devices families use.

Leassh uses SSH-based agents that run natively on macOS. Here's how it works:

The architecture difference:

  • Bark: Agent on device → Data to cloud → AI analysis in cloud → Dashboard in cloud
  • Leassh: Agent on device → Data stays local → Analysis happens locally → Dashboard on your network

What Leassh provides on macOS:

  • Native SSH agent that runs on your Mac
  • Detailed screen time tracking by application
  • Screenshot capture at configurable intervals
  • Complete activity logs visible to parents only
  • No cloud dependency—all data stays on your network
  • Cross-platform consistency (works the same on Mac, Windows, Linux, Android, iOS)

The Privacy Trade-Off Most Parents Don't Consider

When you use cloud-based parental controls like Bark, you're making a significant trade: you get their AI-powered detection, but you give them access to your children's digital life.

Here's what Bark collects from their privacy policy:

  • Screenshots of device screens
  • Social media messages and posts
  • Email content
  • Search history
  • GPS location data
  • Device identifiers

This data is stored on Bark's servers and reviewed by their team. Their privacy policy states they retain this data "for as long as necessary to provide services."

Leassh's approach is different because the data never leaves your home. The SSH agents capture screenshots and screen time data on macOS, Windows, Linux, and Android, but everything stays on devices you control. You're the only one who can access it.

When Bark Might Still Be Right For You

I'm not saying Bark is a bad product. It's genuinely excellent for certain use cases:

Bark works well if:

  • Your family exclusively uses iOS, Windows, Linux, or Android devices
  • You prioritize AI-powered content detection over screen time management
  • You're comfortable with cloud-based data storage
  • You want a proven solution with strong reviews
  • None of your family devices are Macs

Leassh works better if:

  • Your family uses Mac devices (any Macs at all)
  • You want full cross-platform support (Mac, Windows, Linux, Android, iOS)
  • You prioritize privacy and local data storage
  • You want both content monitoring AND advanced screen time controls
  • You're concerned about cloud data collection

The Bottom Line: Check Your Devices Before You Buy

The choice between Bark and Leassh comes down to one question: what devices are you actually trying to protect?

If you have a Mac in your home, Bark's Mac gap means you're paying for features that don't work for your family. That's not just a limitation — it's a fundamental product-market fit problem.

Leassh was built for families like yours: mixed devices, privacy-conscious parents, and teenagers who need monitoring that actually works across everything they own.

What Parents Should Ask Before Buying

Before purchasing any parental control tool, ask these questions:

  1. "Does this work on ALL our family devices?" — Don't buy tools that work on half your devices. Specifically ask about macOS.
  2. "Where is my family's data stored?" — Cloud means someone else has access to your children's digital life.
  3. "Can my child bypass this with a VPN?" — Cloud-based tools can be bypassed locally.
  4. "What happens when my teenager grows up and changes devices?" — Will your tool still work?
  5. "Is there any device my family owns that this can't monitor?" — Be honest about your device mix.

The answers to these questions should guide your decision more than any review or rating.

A Note to Mac Families

If you're reading this and thinking "I've already bought Bark but I have Macs," here's what I recommend:

  1. Contact Bark support — Ask about their macOS roadmap. I'd like to know their answer.
  2. Evaluate your actual monitoring needs — Which devices matter most to you?
  3. Consider alternatives — Leassh offers native Mac support with local privacy.
  4. Don't assume "family" means "all family devices" — Many services don't deliver on that promise.

Parental control is serious business. You're trying to keep your children safe in a digital world that changes every day. The tools you choose should actually work on the devices your family uses.

Don't let a marketing promise create a false sense of security. Verify what your parental control tool can actually see before you trust it with your family's safety.

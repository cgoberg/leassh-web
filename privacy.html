Your data, your rules

We built Leassh so you never have to trust us with your family's data. Here's exactly how it works.

🔒

Local-First Architecture

Leassh runs entirely in your network. Agents connect via SSH to your local dashboard. Screenshots are captured, analyzed by AI running on your hardware, and results stay local. No data ever leaves your home. This is the gold standard of privacy.

Screenshot → Your AI (local) → Results stay local

What we collect

  • ● Periodic screenshots (analyzed locally, never sent to our servers)
  • ● CPU, memory, and disk usage metrics
  • ● Running application names and window titles
  • ● Keyboard and mouse idle time (is someone using the computer?)
  • ● AI-generated activity summaries and categories

What we DON'T collect

  • ✕ No keystrokes. We never record what anyone types.
  • ✕ No browsing history. We don't monitor URLs or search queries.
  • ✕ No file contents. We never read documents, emails, or messages.
  • ✕ No stored screenshots. Screenshots are analyzed locally and never stored on our servers.
  • ✕ No data selling. Your data is never sold, shared, or used for AI training.
  • ✕ No third-party trackers. No Google Analytics, no Facebook pixel, no ad trackers. We use privacy-first Vercel Analytics (cookie-free, anonymized page views only) to understand traffic.

Your data, your rules

We believe parents should be able to understand their children's digital lives without surrendering their family's privacy to yet another company.

That's why Leassh is local-first. The AI runs on your hardware — a laptop, a NAS, even a Raspberry Pi with a GPU. Your screenshots never touch our servers. Your data never leaves your home.

We designed Leassh with one principle: local processing, no retention. Screenshots are analyzed by AI running on your hardware and never stored on our servers. No database. No retention. No audit trail we could be subpoenaed for.

We can't sell what we don't have. We can't be breached if there's nothing to breach. We can't be compelled to hand over data that doesn't exist.

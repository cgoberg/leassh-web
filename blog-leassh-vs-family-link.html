Google Family Link vs Leassh 2026: Which Parental Control Actually Works?

Ingrid Larsen April 10, 2026 10 min read

Google Family Link is free, pre-installed on most Android devices, and marketed to millions of parents. But here’s the thing: it’s a mobile-only tool that requires your child to have a Google account, and it can’t monitor what’s happening on the desktop computers that are increasingly central to education and creative work.

I’m a technologist who built Leassh to solve parental monitoring problems I encountered as a parent. I’ve used Family Link (for my kids’ phones), Qustodio, Bark, and every other tool on the market. Family Link works for what it does. But it’s not the complete solution most families think it is.

Let’s be honest about what Family Link does, what it doesn’t do, and whether Leassh is the right complement for your household.

Google Family Link

Google Family Link
Google ecosystem

Family Link is Google’s parental control solution. It’s free, deeply integrated into Android, and allows parents to create Google accounts for children, approve app downloads, set screen time limits, and remotely lock devices. It’s the default choice for Android parents because it’s free and pre-integrated.

Strengths

  • Completely free — no subscription, no hidden costs
  • Seamless integration with Android and Google Play Store
  • Can remotely lock devices and set bedtimes
  • App approval workflow prevents kids from downloading anything without permission
  • Location tracking on the child’s phone

Limitations

  • Mobile-only: no monitoring of Mac, Linux, or Windows desktops
  • Requires your child to have a Google account (many families already do, but not all)
  • Reports show minutes per app, not what they were doing within those apps
  • All data goes to Google’s servers — no privacy option
  • Can be circumvented by tech-savvy kids who know how to disable the app
Best for: Families with Android phones who want basic screen time management and app approval at no cost. It’s an excellent starting point for mobile monitoring.

Leassh

Leassh
Open, local, transparent

Leassh monitors what applications are running on your child’s devices using SSH (the standard remote access protocol built into Mac and Linux). No app needs to be installed on the target devices. It sees process-level detail: which applications were running, at what times, in what sequence. The key differentiator: weekly narrative reports that tell a story about what your child has been working on.

Strengths

  • Desktop monitoring on Mac, Linux, and Windows (via OpenSSH)
  • Local privacy option — monitoring data can stay on your home network
  • Process-level detail: not just “4 hours on computer” but which applications, at what times
  • Weekly narrative reports that tell a story about what your child has been working on
  • No app installation required on target devices (uses existing SSH)
  • Works in mixed-OS households (Mac + Linux + Windows)

Limitations

  • Not designed for mobile device monitoring — focus is on desktop/laptop
  • More technical to set up than Family Link’s “install the app” flow
  • Requires SSH access to be enabled on target devices (usually on by default on Mac)
  • Not free — subscription-based (but includes behavioral understanding features)
Best for: Families who need desktop monitoring, who value privacy and local data, or who run mixed-OS households (especially those with Linux machines). Also useful for homelabbers with shared GPU fleets.

The real question: Do you need desktop monitoring?

Family Link is excellent for mobile. But here’s the problem: most educational and creative work happens on desktops and laptops, not phones. A kid can spend 6 hours on a computer learning to code, building 3D models in Blender, or researching for school — and Family Link will tell you nothing about it.

The gap: If you’re using Family Link, you know how long your child used their phone. But you have no visibility into what they were doing on their Chromebook, MacBook, or Windows laptop. That’s where the majority of homework, creative projects, and increasingly, social interaction happens.

Why desktop monitoring matters

I’ve talked to dozens of parents who discovered their child was spending hours on something entirely different than expected — or, more commonly, something far more interesting than “playing games.” My own son spent weeks building Minecraft tutorials and teaching himself Scratch. Family Link would have shown “4 hours on computer” and I would have had no idea it was him learning. Leassh told me he was working in Scratch, which led to a conversation about what he was building.

The difference between “4 hours on the computer” and “4 hours building a Scratch animation” changes how I respond as a parent. One triggers a conversation about time management. The other triggers a conversation about creativity and learning.

Can Family Link and Leassh work together?

Yes. In fact, for many families, that’s the ideal setup:

  • Family Link on mobile: Free, effective for phone monitoring, app approval, and location tracking
  • Leassh on desktop: Monitors Mac, Linux, and Windows devices without installing anything

This combination gives you complete coverage across all device types, with Leassh providing the behavioral understanding that Family Link lacks for desktop devices.

The privacy question

Family Link sends all monitoring data to Google&rsquo servers. Your child’s app usage, location, screen time, and device activity are all stored in Google’s cloud. This is fine for many families, but privacy-conscious parents often want an option where monitoring data stays on their home network.

Leassh is the only parental control tool I’ve found that offers a local-network option. Your monitoring data never leaves your home unless you choose to enable cloud sync. For families who’ve already chosen Leassh for its monitoring capabilities, this local option is a significant differentiator.

Technical requirements

Family Link setup

  • Android device for the child (iOS support is limited)
  • Google account for parent and child
  • Install the Family Link app on both devices
  • Configure limits and approvals through the parent app

Leassh setup

  • SSH access enabled on target devices (usually on by default on Mac)
  • One-time configuration of SSH credentials in Leassh
  • Leassh agent runs locally and queries devices via SSH
  • No software installation required on target devices

If your family already uses SSH for anything (homelab, remote work, etc.), Leassh is essentially “turn it on and configure it.” If you’ve never used SSH, there’s a learning curve — but it’s a standard protocol that’s been around for decades.

Cost comparison

Family Link is free. Leassh is a paid service (see pricing page for current plans).

Here’s how I think about it: Family Link is a mobile monitoring tool that happens to be free. Leassh is a behavioral understanding tool that happens to be paid. They’re solving different problems at different price points.

If you’re paying for parental control software anyway (Qustodio, Bark, Net Nanny), Leassh is often cheaper and provides more meaningful data. If you’re currently using Family Link for mobile and need desktop coverage, Leassh is a cost-effective complement.

What I’d actually recommend

For families with Android phones and no desktop monitoring needs: Family Link is an excellent starting point. It’s free, effective, and covers the mobile use case better than any paid alternative.

For families who need desktop monitoring: Family Link won’t help. You need Leassh, or you need a different desktop-focused solution. (Most commercial tools have weak desktop support compared to Leassh.)

For mixed-OS households (Mac + Linux + Windows): Leassh is essentially the only practical option. Family Link can’t monitor Linux at all, and Windows support in most parental control tools is an afterthought.

For privacy-conscious families: Leassh’s local option means monitoring data never leaves your home. Family Link, Qustodio, Bark, and Net Nanny all require cloud storage.

The honest truth

Family Link is a good tool for what it does. It’s free, it works, and for mobile monitoring, it’s hard to beat. But it’s not a complete solution for modern families who need desktop monitoring.

Leassh fills that gap. It doesn’t try to be a mobile monitoring tool. It’s focused on what Family Link can’t do: seeing what’s happening on desktop computers, providing behavioral understanding through narrative reports, and offering local privacy options.

The best outcome from any monitoring tool is a parent walking in and saying “I saw you were working in Scratch — what were you building?” — not one who silently enforces limits from a dashboard. Leassh tries to make that first sentence more informed.

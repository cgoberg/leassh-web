Net Nanny vs Leassh: Why Android Support Still Matters in 2026

Ivar Nielsen April 7, 2026

Net Nanny has been promising Android support for years. As of 2026, they still can't monitor Android devices. For a parental control company that's been around since 1996, this isn't just a feature gap — it's a fundamental misunderstanding of how modern families use technology.

Here's what that means for parents trying to choose between Net Nanny and genuinely cross-platform alternatives like Leassh.

The Android Gap That Won't Close

According to multiple 2026 reviews and direct communication with Net Nanny support, Net Nanny still has no Android child monitoring app. Their support team has confirmed they don't have a specific release date, despite promising Android compatibility "soon" for over two years.

What Net Nanny does have is a parent management app for Android phones — so parents can adjust settings and view reports from their own Android devices. But if your child uses an Android phone, tablet, or Chromebook, Net Nanny can't monitor it.

The Bottom Line: In 2026, if your family has any Android devices that children use, Net Nanny simply isn't an option. Full stop.

Why This Actually Matters

Android holds roughly 71% of the global smartphone market. In many regions, it's over 80%. More importantly, Android devices are often the more affordable option for families buying multiple devices — exactly the situation where parental controls become essential.

Consider these common family scenarios where Net Nanny's Android limitation becomes a dealbreaker:

  • Mixed households: Dad has an iPhone, Mom has Android, kids have budget Android tablets
  • Chromebook families: Many schools issue Chromebooks; kids often have personal Chromebooks too
  • Hand-me-down phones: Older Android phones becoming kids' first smartphones
  • Budget-conscious parents: Android devices offer better value at every price point

In any of these situations, Net Nanny creates an artificial limitation: you either replace your Android devices with iOS ones (expensive) or accept that some devices simply can't be monitored (defeats the purpose).

What Net Nanny Does Well (When It Works)

To be fair to Net Nanny, on the platforms it does support, it's genuinely effective:

  • Web filtering: Among the most sophisticated content blocking engines available
  • Real-time protection: Blocks inappropriate content as it loads, not just by category
  • Long track record: 30 years of development means stability and reliability
  • Simple setup: Installation and configuration is straightforward

For families that are entirely Windows, macOS, and iOS, Net Nanny can be an effective web filtering tool. The problem is that "entirely Windows, macOS, and iOS" describes fewer and fewer households every year.

How Leassh Handles Cross-Platform Monitoring

Leassh takes a different technical approach that makes true cross-platform support possible: instead of requiring platform-specific apps, it works through SSH connections that exist on all modern operating systems.

This means:

  • No Android app needed: Monitoring works through the Linux subsystem that Android runs on
  • Consistent features: The same behavioral reports and screen time tracking work identically across all platforms
  • Future-proof: New devices and operating systems don't require separate development cycles
  • Local option: All monitoring data can stay on your home network, unlike cloud-dependent solutions

The trade-off is setup complexity. Leassh requires more technical knowledge to configure than Net Nanny's click-to-install approach. For families comfortable with that trade-off, it removes the artificial platform limitations entirely.

What This Means for Your Family

The choice between Net Nanny and Leassh often comes down to a simple question: Do you need to monitor Android devices?

If your answer is yes, Net Nanny isn't viable. If your answer is "not now, but maybe in the future," you're betting that Net Nanny will finally deliver Android support before your family needs it — a bet they've been asking customers to make for several years now.

If your family is genuinely iOS and desktop-only, and web filtering is your primary concern, Net Nanny's strength in content blocking might outweigh its platform limitations.

But for most families in 2026, buying a parental control solution that can't monitor the world's most popular mobile operating system feels like buying a car that only drives on Sundays — technically functional, but missing the point.

Leassh vs Apple Screen Time (2026): A Better Alternative for Families?

By Ingrid Svendsen • April 2026

Apple Screen Time is free and built into every iOS and macOS device. If you have an all-Apple household—iPhones, iPads, Macs—it might seem like the complete monitoring solution you need.

But here's the reality: Screen Time only works within Apple's walled garden.

If your kids use anything else—a Windows PC for schoolwork, a Linux laptop, an Android tablet, even a Chromebook from school—Screen Time gives you nothing. You're left staring at an incomplete picture of their digital lives, and that incomplete picture is exactly where problems hide.

What Is Apple Screen Time?

Screen Time is Apple's parental control tool for monitoring device usage on iOS and macOS. Here's what it can do:

  • App usage tracking: See how much time is spent in apps and categories
  • Website history: View visited websites and app usage per device
  • Downtime schedules: Set times when the device is locked
  • App limits: Restrict certain apps after a daily time limit
  • Content restrictions: Filter by content rating and restrict explicit content
  • Location sharing: See where your children are on a map

For Apple-only families, this covers the basics. But the moment you add a second type of device, the system falls apart.

The Critical Gap: The Multi-Device Problem

Most families in 2026 are multi-device households. Your kids might use:

  • An iPad for homework and gaming
  • A Mac for schoolwork and video editing
  • A Windows PC or Chromebook (loaned by school)
  • A smartphone (probably iPhone, but sometimes Android)
  • A gaming console (Nintendo, PlayStation, Xbox)

Screen Time monitors three of these: iPhone, iPad, and Mac. For the rest—Windows, Linux, Android, Chromebooks, gaming consoles—you get nothing.

I've watched countless Apple parents realize, too late, that their kids were spending 5+ hours daily on the school-issued Chromebook they thought was just for "a few assignments."

The bottom line: If your child uses even one non-Apple device regularly, Screen Time leaves a blind spot in your monitoring.

Screen Time Limitations (Even on Apple Devices)

Even within the Apple ecosystem, Screen Time has some important limitations:

Limited Behavioral Insights

Screen Time tells you how long your kids use apps, but not what they're actually doing with them. Did your child spend 2 hours on YouTube watching educational coding tutorials, or 2 hours watching inappropriate content? Screen Time doesn't distinguish.

You get categories like "Social Networking," "Entertainment," and "Productivity," but no visibility into actual behavior.

Children Can Often Bypass Restrictions

Kids find workarounds. Some older teens know how to:

  • Change the device time zone to bypass Downtime schedules
  • Reset the device and remove parental controls (on older macOS versions)
  • Use personal hotspots to bypass screen time restrictions
  • Request app time extensions (and the pressure to approve them is real for parents)

Cross-Device Visibility is a Nightmare

Screen Time shows you activity per device, not per child across devices. If you have three kids and five Apple devices, you need to check multiple screens to get a complete picture. One child might look fine on the iPad but be overspending on the iPhone.

No Actionable Behavioral Reporting

You can see that your child used "Social Networking" for 4 hours. But why? Are they in a group chat with schoolmates? Scrolling TikTok? Being cyberbullied? Screen Time doesn't say.

When Screen Time Is Enough

If you check all these boxes, Screen Time might be sufficient:

  • ✓ All your kids use only Apple devices (iPhone, iPad, Mac)
  • ✓ They never borrow school-issued laptops or use public computers
  • ✓ You don't care about seeing what's actually on their screens (just time spent)
  • ✓ You trust that app categorization gives you enough context
  • ✓ Your kids have never tried to bypass parental controls

For strict all-Apple households with younger children, Screen Time is a reasonable baseline.

When Leassh Is Worth It

Leassh makes sense if:

  • ✓ Your kids use a mix of devices (Mac, Windows, iPad, Android, Chromebook, etc.)
  • ✓ You want to see what's actually happening, not just category totals
  • ✓ You want a single dashboard to monitor all children across all devices
  • ✓ You care about behavioral patterns—not just "how long," but "what and why"
  • ✓ Your kids are old enough to try bypassing restrictions, and you want something harder to circumvent
  • ✓ You want to see actual website URLs, not just "social networking"
  • ✓ You want periodic screenshots to understand what they're doing

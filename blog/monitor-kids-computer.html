How to Monitor Kids Computer in 2026: A Parent's Guide to Real Visibility

By Ingrid Svendsen • Updated April 2026

You're here because you've asked the question every parent eventually faces: how do I actually monitor what my kid is doing on their computer?

Google that question, and you'll find hundreds of answers. Cloud-based parental controls. Free built-in tools. Subscription services promising "comprehensive monitoring." It's overwhelming. And worse, most of it doesn't actually tell you what you need to know.

I've spent the last two years helping parents set up monitoring for their kids across Macs, Windows machines, and mixed-device households. I've tested cloud solutions, free tools, and self-hosted systems. Here's what I've learned about what real computer monitoring looks like in 2026—and why the most effective approach keeps your family's data in your home.

What "Monitoring" Actually Means (And What Doesn't)

Before diving into tools, let's be clear about what parents actually need when they ask how to monitor kids computer:

  1. Specific app usage — Not "2 hours of browsing," but "31 minutes on YouTube, 18 minutes on Discord"
  2. Visual verification — Screenshots that show what's actually on screen
  3. Content visibility — What websites, what messages, what videos
  4. Historical tracking — Patterns over days and weeks, not just today's summary
  5. Cross-device consolidation — One place to see everything, regardless of platform

Most tools claim to do all of this. Few actually deliver. And the ones that do often send all that intimate data to a third party's cloud.

The Cloud Monitoring Trap

Services like Bark, Qustodio, and Net Nanny are popular for a reason: they're easy to install. Download an app, create an account, connect your kid's device. Done.

But here's what happens next: every screenshot, every timestamp, every app name gets uploaded to their servers.

Your child's browsing history. Their screen captures. How long they spend on each activity. All stored on someone else's infrastructure, governed by their privacy policy, potentially accessible to their employees, potentially vulnerable to breaches.

Read Bark's privacy policy. They admit: "we cannot ensure or guarantee the security of your Data and expressly disclaim any such obligation." That's not just legal boilerplate—that's a parent reading that and realizing their kid's data isn't actually secure.

Key question: Would you trust a stranger with your child's diary? If not, why trust them with screenshots of their screen?

The "Free" Alternative Problem

Microsoft Family Safety. Apple Screen Time. Google Family Link. They're free, they're built into your devices, and on paper they sound great.

But they're designed for basic control, not real monitoring. Let's be specific:

Microsoft Family Safety tells you "your kid spent 2 hours on games." It does not tell you which game. It does not show you what they were doing during those 2 hours. It's screen time counting, not monitoring.

Apple Screen Time is slightly better—it breaks down by app category. But you still don't get visual verification. You don't get to see what's on screen. You get categories, not content.

Google Family Link works well for Chromebooks and Android devices, but it's cloud-based and doesn't cover Mac or Windows computers in a household.

The pattern is consistent: free tools count minutes. They don't show you what matters.

How to Set Up Computer Monitoring (Step by Step)

Ready to move beyond basic tools? Here's what the Leassh setup looks like:

Step 1: Set Up the Dashboard Server

The dashboard runs locally on a machine in your home. This could be:

  • Your main computer (if you're okay with it running in the background)
  • A dedicated machine like a Raspberry Pi ($35-50)
  • An old laptop you have lying around

You download the Leassh server binary, run a configuration file, and it starts listening for agent connections. The entire process takes about 10 minutes if you follow the documentation.

Step 2: Install Agents on Each Computer

For each computer you want to monitor:

  • Windows: Download the portable agent executable, run it once
  • Mac: Download the agent app, grant accessibility permissions when prompted
  • Linux: Download the agent binary, make it executable, run it

Each agent connects to your dashboard via SSH (encrypted, secure) and starts reporting screen time data.

Step 3: Configure Capture Settings

Decide how often you want screenshots captured. The default is every 5 minutes, but you can adjust this based on your needs. More frequent = more detail, but more storage. Less frequent = lighter footprint, but potentially missing important moments.

Step 4: Review in Your Local Dashboard

Open the dashboard in your browser (it runs on localhost or your local network). You'll see:

  • Real-time screen time breakdown by app
  • Historical charts showing patterns over days and weeks
  • A gallery of screenshots you can browse chronologically
  • Activity logs showing what happened when

Everything is stored locally. You decide how long to keep it. You decide who can access it (spoiler: it's just you, the parent).

The Technical Side (Simplified)

If you're reading this and thinking "okay, but what's SSH?"—here's the short version:

SSH is a secure protocol for remote connections. It's been around for decades, it's battle-tested, and it's encrypted end-to-end. When Leassh agents connect to your dashboard via SSH, they're using the same protocol that system administrators have used securely for 30+ years.
